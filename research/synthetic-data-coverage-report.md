## Question 2: Coverage Design (Sampling the Generation Space)

**Verdict:** ✅ Pairwise (t=2) covering arrays via NIST ACTS; supplement with t=3 for high-risk degradation strata

### Sources
| Source | Year | Cites | DOI | Key Finding |
|--------|------|-------|-----|-------------|
| **Kuhn et al.** "Software Fault Interactions" (IEEE TSE) | 2004 | 608 | `10.1109/tse.2004.24` | **Core evidence**: ~70% failures triggered by 1 parameter, ~90% by 2-way, ~98% by 3-way interactions. Beyond 3-way, incremental gain near zero. |
| **NIST SP 800-142** "Practical Combinatorial Testing" | 2010 | — | `10.6028/NIST.SP.800-142` | Official NIST tutorial on t-way testing; covering array generation (IPO/IPOG), variable-strength arrays, constraint handling |
| **ACTS tool** (Yu et al., ICST 2013, Most Influential Paper) | 2013 | 143 | `10.1109/icst.2013.52` | **4,500+ users**. Free, public domain, Java (GUI+CLI). Generates covering arrays from text-based factor models. GitHub: `github.com/usnistgov/combinatorial-testing-tools` (78★) |
| "Bypassing the Combinatorial Explosion" (Henard, TSE) | 2014 | 214 | `10.1109/tse.2014.2327020` | Similarity-based heuristics for t-wise when covering arrays too large |
| "Test them all, is it worth it?" (Halin, EMSE) | 2019 | 114 | `10.1007/s10664-018-9635-4` | **Pairwise caught most config faults** on JHipster. Higher t gave marginal returns. Random sampling competitive at sufficient density |
| "Empirical Comparison: CT vs RT vs ART" (Wu, TSE) | 2018 | 62 | `10.1109/tse.2018.2852744` | CT best overall. Random competitive for low t. ART only helps when oracle expensive (not your case) |

### Method Most Transferable

**Recommended: t-way covering arrays, t=2 (pairwise), with variable-strength t=3 for high-risk cells.**

Comparison across sampling strategies for your factor space (example: content=10, style=5, renderer=4, degradation=8):

| Method | Interaction Guarantee | # Tests | Best For |
|--------|---------------------|---------|----------|
| Full factorial | All | 1,600 | Infeasible |
| **Pairwise (t=2) covering array** | ✅ All 2-way combos | ~80–200 | **Your primary method** |
| t=3 covering array | ✅ All 3-way combos | ~400–600 | Degradation × content high-risk cells |
| Stratified sampling | ❌ No interaction guarantee | User-chosen | Naive baseline |
| Latin hypercube | ❌ Only 1D projection | User-chosen | Continuous parameters, not categorical |
| Random sampling | ❌ Probabilistic | User-chosen | Cheap baseline |

**Implementation steps:**
1. Model your space as an ACTS parameter-relation table (categorical factors with discrete levels)
2. Generate pairwise covering array: `java -jar acts.jar -Dmodel=model.txt -Dt=2`
3. Add variable-strength t=3 constraints for degradation+content+renderer triples
4. Use ACTS combinatorial coverage measurement to verify coverage achieved

### Warnings / Gaps
- ⚠️ **Kuhn's result is empirical, not a theorem.** It was measured on traditional enterprise/embedded software, NOT image pipelines. Degradation × content × renderer interactions may follow a different profile. Budget for t=3 spot checks.
- ⚠️ **Covering arrays guarantee t-way coverage, not failure detection.** A passing t-way test means every combination of parameter values appeared, not that the extractor works correctly on them.
- ⚠️ **Categorical factors require grouping.** If ContentParams has 500 values, pairwise grows large. Group into equivalence classes first.
- ⚠️ **ACTS is Java-only.** Requires JRE. No maintained Python wrapper — shell out to CLI or write a thin subprocess wrapper.

## Question 3: Coverage Measurement

**Verdict:** ⚠️ Subagent dispatched for detailed analysis

### Sources Located (Preliminary)
| Source | Year | Cites | Key Finding |
|--------|------|-------|-------------|
| "Cats are not fish" (Humphreys et al., ICSE) | 2020 | 57 | OOD-aware DL testing; shows many "errors" found by testing tools are actually OOD samples, not bugs |
| "Machine Learning Testing: Survey" (2019) | 2019 | 108 | Comprehensive survey of DL coverage metrics |
| Branch coverage for non-neural pipelines | — | — | Classic software testing: branch/line coverage is the proven proxy |

### Method Most Transferable
For a deterministic pipeline, the appropriate coverage metrics are:

1. **Code-branch coverage** of extractor source code — use `coverage.py` or gcov. This is the simplest, most interpretable metric. Every uncovered branch is a potential failure mode.
2. **Input-space partition coverage** — divide each factor (renderer, chart type, data distribution) into equivalence classes and measure what fraction of (class A, class B, ...) combinations have been tested.
3. **Combinatorial coverage measurement** — after testing, measure what fraction of t-way parameter interactions were actually exercised. NIST provides `CCM` (Combinatorial Coverage Measurement) tool.

### Why NOT neuron coverage
- **Neuron coverage** (NC, KMNC, etc.) applies only to neural network activations. For deterministic code, **branch coverage** is the direct analogue and is more meaningful.
- Negative result: neuron coverage correlates POORLY with fault detection (see "Cats are not fish" — many found errors are OOD, not real faults). For deterministic code, the correlation between branch coverage and fault detection is well-established.
- The critique of neuron coverage (it's easy to achieve high NC without meaningful testing) is a warning against any coverage metric that can be gamed without exercising real variation.

### Warnings / Gaps
- ⚠️ **Branch coverage is necessary but not sufficient.** Your extractors may have high branch coverage from synthetic tests but still fail on images with properties not encoded in code logic (e.g., specific font rendering artifacts).
- ⚠️ **No established analogue of "input coverage" for deterministic image pipelines.** You will need to define your own partition scheme per vertical (bar chart vs. circuit schematic vs. free-body diagram).

## Question 4: Oracle-Guided / Adversarial Generation

**Verdict:** ✅ Boundary-value sweep + mutation around thresholds beats metamorphic testing when you have an exact oracle. Two-phase strategy.

### Sources
| Source | Year | Cites | DOI | Key Finding |
|--------|------|-------|-----|-------------|
| **DeepTest** (Tian et al., ICSE) | 2018 | 1227 | `10.1145/3180155.3180220` | Metamorphic testing for autonomous driving; image transformations (rain, fog, brightness) as relations. **Limitation:** assumes NO oracle — you have one |
| **"The Oracle Problem"** (Barr et al., TSE) | 2015 | 1043 | `10.1109/tse.2014.2372785` | Definitive oracle taxonomy. Your system = **specified oracle** (ground truth from renderer) — the "easy" case |
| **"ML Testing: Survey"** (Zhang, TSE) | 2020 | 578 | `10.1109/tse.2019.2962027` | Comprehensive ML testing survey. Distinguishes oracle-based (correctness) vs no-oracle (robustness) testing |
| "Test Selection for DLS" (Ma, TOSEM) | 2021 | 100 | `10.1145/3417330` | Diversity-based selection transfers; neuron coverage does not |
| "Metamorphic Testing: Review" (Segura, ACM CS) | 2018 | — | Search: "Segura metamorphic testing survey" | MT applied mainly to numerical/scientific software and DNNs. Image processing underrepresented |

### Method Most Transferable

**Two-phase strategy: boundary-value sweep → mutation-driven adversarial generation.**

Because your system has CHEAP EXACT ORACLES (unlike virtually all ML testing literature):

**Phase 1: Boundary-Value Sweep (Systematic Coverage)**
For each degradation parameter, sweep values at fine granularity around failure boundaries:

| Parameter | Sweep Range | Expected Failure Zone |
|-----------|-------------|----------------------|
| Gaussian blur σ | [0, 0.5, 1.0, 1.5, 2.0, 2.5, 3.0, 4.0, 6.0, 10.0] | OCR fails ~σ>1.5 |
| Noise (σ) | [0, 0.01, 0.02, 0.05, 0.1, 0.15, 0.2, 0.3, 0.5] | Template match ~>0.1 |
| Rotation ° | [0, 0.5, 1, 2, 3, 5, 10, 15] | Template match ~>3° |
| Brightness Δ | [0, ±10, ±30, ±50, ±80, ±100] | OCR fails at extremes |

Run each sweep on a reference image (selected via t-way covering array from Q2). Since oracle is exact:
- Automatically detect exact parameter value where extractor fails
- Classify failure as **abrupt cliff** (deterministic) or **gradual slope** (non-deterministic)

**Phase 2: Mutation-Driven Adversarial (Around Discovered Thresholds)**
When Phase 1 finds a failure threshold, map the failure surface via local grid:

```
Example: blur σ threshold at 1.5 → local grid:
  blur: [1.3, 1.4, 1.5, 1.6, 1.7]
  blur + noise: [noise=0, 0.01, 0.02]
  blur + resolution: [150, 200, 300] dpi
```

This creates a **failure region map** — add centroid as permanent regression test.

### Key Insight: Why NOT Metamorphic Testing as Primary Strategy
- Metamorphic testing is designed for **no-oracle** scenarios (check consistency relations instead). You have an exact oracle. Comparing `f(x)` to `ground_truth(x)` is stronger and cheaper.
- Use metamorphic testing ONLY as supplemental sanity check: e.g., 2x rescale round-trip should return same extractor output. This catches bugs in shared code (renderer + extractor share subroutines → oracle is blind to shared bugs).

### Warnings / Gaps
- ⚠️ **Most ML testing literature targets DNNs — its key assumptions don't transfer.** DNN testing concerns (opacity, no oracle, neuron coverage) are not your problem. Do NOT adopt DeepTest-style neuron coverage search strategies.
- ⚠️ **Oracle is cheap but verifying the oracle is not trivial.** If renderer and extractor share subroutines (same font rasterizer), shared bugs produce identical wrong output → oracle is blind. Cross-check with a second implementation.
- ⚠️ **Failure regions are extractor-specific.** A threshold for template matching (rotation > 3°) has no bearing on OCR (which may work at 45°). Each extractor type needs its own sweep.
- ⚠️ **Deterministic extractors have sharp failure boundaries** (unlike DNNs with gradual degradation). You need fine-grained sweeps near expected thresholds, not coarse uniform sampling.
- ⚠️ **Beware overfitting regression tests.** Run each sweep point ≥3 times; take median threshold. Single-run measurements may include non-deterministic noise.

### Augraphy Capabilities (Verified from GitHub Description)
- Paper texture simulation
- Ink bleed / photocopy effects
- Folding lines, creases
- Staple holes, binder holes
- Fax transmission artifacts
- Scanner noise, moiré patterns
- JPEG compression artifacts
- Lighting gradient, non-uniform illumination
- Dirty/soiled paper
- Page rotation, border removal artifacts

### Critical Gap: Ground-Truth Geometry
- **PIXEL-LEVEL degradations** (blur, noise, JPEG, brightness) do NOT change ground-truth coordinates → safe for extractor testing
- **GEOMETRIC degradations** (perspective warp, paper curl, rotation) DO change coordinates → ground truth must be transformed identically
- Augraphy and ocrodeg were designed for OCR/document classification, NOT for geometry-preserving extractor testing. **You must apply the same geometric transform to your ground-truth annotations** if you use warping transformations.
- **Screenshot-of-screenshot** is NOT directly modeled by Augraphy but can be approximated by: render → save → reload → apply degradation → re-encode

### Most Transferable Method
- Use Augraphy as your primary degradation pipeline (most comprehensive, Python-native)
- For each degradation family, classify as **geometry-preserving** (blur, noise, jpeg, brightness) vs **geometry-transforming** (warp, perspective, rotation)
- For geometry-transforming degradations, apply the EXACT SAME transform to ground-truth coordinate maps
- For screenshot realism: render at native resolution → save as PNG → apply degradation → save as JPEG

### Warnings / Gaps
- ⚠️ **Augraphy's moiré simulation is untested for chart/diagram content.** Moiré patterns depend on spatial frequencies in the image — charts with fine gridlines or dense text may produce moiré differently than general documents.
- ⚠️ **No validated degradation model for AI-generated images.** If your system must handle AI-generated diagrams (DALL-E, Midjourney, etc.), none of these degradation pipelines have been validated for that domain.
- ⚠️ **"Realism" is not the same as "test difficulty."** An unrealistic degradation may still stress-test your extractors effectively. Don't over-optimize for visual realism when coverage is the goal.

For YOUR deterministic extractor system, the relevant insight is:

1. **LLM generates content templates** → you render deterministically → extractors must parse. This is the "Synthesize-Step-by-Step" paradigm adapted for verification.
2. **The value of LLM over random template** is in generating REALISTIC content that exercises edge cases:
   - Natural label distributions (not just uniform "A,B,C,D")
   - Realistic data patterns (trends with noise, outliers, missing values)
   - Multi-locale number formats (1,234.56 vs 1.234,56)
   - Typical overlapping label scenarios
   - Natural-language axis titles (not just "X-axis" / "Y-axis")

### Content-Realism Factors That Matter
For verification of deterministic extractors, these content dimensions matter most:
- **Label collision density** — how often labels overlap/squish
- **Value precision** — integer vs float vs scientific notation
- **Data distribution shape** — uniform, normal, lognormal, discrete, empty
- **Real-world naming** — domain-specific names vs placeholder text
- **Missing/irregular data** — NaN, outliers, empty series

### Warnings / Gaps
- ⚠️ **No published "taxonomy of chart content realism"** exists in the literature. You would need to build this.
- ⚠️ LLM-generated content may have **distributional biases** (mode collapse, round-number preference) that reduce the very diversity you're seeking. Validate LLM output distribution before use.
- ⚠️ For deterministic extractors, **template-based generation with systematic perturbation is MORE COVERAGE-EFFICIENT** than LLM generation for known corner cases. Reserve LLM generation for the UNKNOWN corners you can't anticipate.

## Question 7: Selective-Prediction Evaluation

**Verdict:** ✅ Strong literature with direct transferability

### Sources
| Source | Year | Cites | Key Finding |
|--------|------|-------|-------------|
| **SelectiveNet** (Geifman & El-Yaniv, NeurIPS) | 2019 | — | Deep neural network with integrated reject option; formalizes selective prediction framework |
| Chow (1970, IEEE TIT) | 1970 | — | **Origin of reject option**: optimal error-reject tradeoff for Bayes classifiers |
| **Wilson** (1927, JASA) | 1927 | 3744 | "Probable Inference, the Law of Succession, and Statistical Inference" — origin of Wilson score interval for binomial proportions |
| "Selective Question Answering under Domain Shift" (Kamath et al., ACL) | 2020 | 123 | Selective QA evaluation: abstain when uncertain |
| "Second opinion needed" (Vasey et al., npj Digital Medicine) | 2021 | 444 | Communicating uncertainty in medical ML: selective prediction in practice |
| "Evaluating pointwise reliability of ML prediction" (2022) | 2022 | 93 | Reliability estimation for individual predictions |

### Method Most Transferable

**A. Selective Prediction Framework**
Your system's pass/fail/needs_review maps directly to:
- **decided** cases: pass or fail (binary prediction)
- **abstained** cases: needs_review (reject option)

The canonical evaluation is the **risk-coverage curve**: plot risk (error rate on decided cases) against coverage (fraction of cases decided). A good system achieves low risk at high coverage.

Key metrics:
- **Coverage at target risk** — e.g., "95% coverage at 1% risk"
- **AURC** (Area Under Risk-Coverage Curve) — single-number summary
- **Selective risk at fixed coverage** — e.g., risk at 90% coverage

**B. Statistical Honesty for Small n**
For per-stratum reporting (n = 20-50 test cases per vertical):

- **Wilson score interval** (Wilson 1927): the recommended confidence interval for binomial proportions (better coverage than Wald). For k failures in n tests: compute ±CI using Wilson formula.
- **Rule of three**: if 0 failures observed in n tests, the 95% upper bound on failure rate is 3/n. E.g., 0/30 → 95% CI: [0, 0.100].
- **Clopper-Pearson exact interval**: conservative alternative for very small n.
- **Recommendation**: Report each stratum as "accuracy-at-coverage: X% [Wilson CI: low–high] at Y% coverage."

**C. For Your System**
For a deterministic system, the selective predictor is built from EXTRACTOR CONFIDENCE (not model probability). Specific approaches:

1. **Distance-to-model-boundary**: for template matching, accept if match score > threshold
2. **Ensemble disagreement**: run 2+ extractors (e.g., different template libraries); if they disagree, abstain
3. **Cross-renderer consistency**: render same spec → extract from N rendered versions → if results disagree, abstain
4. **Metadata validation**: extract results → validate against spec constraints → if constraints violated, abstain

### Warnings / Gaps
- ⚠️ SelectiveNet and most selective prediction literature assumes a LEARNED confidence estimator (a neural network). YOUR system must use heuristic/engineered confidence scores. **Validate that your confidence score is well-calibrated** (use reliability diagrams) — this is NOT guaranteed and must be tested.
- ⚠️ **The "coverage" of selective prediction is different from the "coverage" of your generation space.** Selective prediction = fraction of CASES decided; generation coverage = fraction of INPUT SPACE explored. Both matter but are distinct metrics.
- ⚠️ **Small-n statistics require honest reporting.** If n=25 and 0 failures: report as "0 failures [95% CI: 0–12%]" — do NOT report as "100% accurate."

## Question 8: Structural / Grammar-Based Generation

**Verdict:** ✅ Renderer's own DSL as grammar is the practical path; build not bought

### Sources Located (Preliminary)
| Source | Key Approach |
|--------|-------------|
| **Mermaid.js grammar** | Declarative DSL for flowcharts, sequence diagrams, class diagrams, Gantt charts. Input is a structured text grammar. Output is SVG/PNG. |
| **Graphviz DOT language** | Grammar for directed/undirected graphs. Highly expressive for network-like diagrams. |
| **TikZ/PGF** (LaTeX graphics) | Turing-complete graphics language. Can generate anything but hardest to enumerate. |
| **Shape grammars** (Stiny, 1972+) | Generative design via shape rules; used in architecture, CAD, product design |
| **Graph grammars** | Formal grammars for graph languages; node+edge rewriting rules |

### Most Transferable Method

**A. Renderer's own input format as grammar**
This is the most practical insight: each renderer already has a well-defined input format that acts as a grammar. Use it:
- **Mermaid DSL** → generate flowcharts with topological variations → render → extract
- **Graphviz DOT** → generate graph topologies (trees, DAGs, cycles) → render → extract
- **TikZ templates** → parameterize to generate variations → render → extract

**B. Grammar-based generation for schematics/circuits**
- Circuit schematics can be generated using graph grammars where:
  - Nodes = components (resistors, capacitors, gates)
  - Edges = connections
  - Production rules = valid circuit topologies
- Free-body force diagrams can be generated via a vector grammar:
  - Node = object/point
  - Arrow = force vector (with direction + magnitude)
  - Rules = Newton's laws as topological constraints

**C. Topological coverage**
- **Graph motif coverage**: test set should cover star motifs, chain motifs, cycle motifs, branching motifs
- **Graph property coverage**: planar vs non-planar, directed vs undirected, rooted vs unrooted, tree vs DAG vs cyclic
- For 20 node types × 5 connectivity patterns × 3 size scales = thorough coverage in ~50-100 graphs

### Warnings / Gaps
- ⚠️ **No off-the-shelf tool for grammar-based diagram generation with exact ground truth**. You must build this yourself, but the building blocks exist (Mermaid DSL, Graphviz, TikZ, NetworkX).
- ⚠️ **Grammar design is domain-specific.** A flowchart grammar is very different from a circuit grammar. Each vertical needs its own grammar.
- ⚠️ **Topological coverage ≠ functional coverage.** A flowchart with every graph structure may still lack realistic labels or proper edge routing.

## Recommended Build Order

**Verdict:** ✅ Synthesized from all findings

### Top 3 Coverage-per-Effort Investments (Solo Developer)

**Investment #1: Pairwise covering array + scripted generation harness (Weeks 1–3)**
- **What:** Model your generation space as categorical factors (Renderers, ChartTypes, ContentClasses, StyleVariants, DegradationTypes). Use `itertools.combinations` or a Python covering-array script to generate pairwise test specs. Write a single generation harness that takes a spec tuple → produces rendered image + ground-truth JSON.
- **Why #1:** This gives you the highest coverage-per-test-case ratio. Pairwise covers all 2-way interactions (which catch ~70-90% of failures per Kuhn). Scripted generation means you add renderers by writing one driver per tool.
- **Effort:** ~3 days per renderer integration, plus 1 day for the array generator and ground-truth encoder.
- **Evidence:** Kuhn NIST findings on interaction strength; direct applicability to categorical factor spaces.

**Investment #2: Branch-coverage-guided test augmentation (Weeks 3–4)**
- **What:** Instrument your extractors with `coverage.py`. Run your initial test suite. For each uncovered branch, manually analyze: "what input would exercise this branch?" Add targeted test cases. Iterate until branch coverage > 90%.
- **Why #2:** This catches the REAL failure modes of YOUR code, not theoretical failure modes of the application domain. Deterministic extractors have bounded complexity — branch coverage is both the metric and the guide.
- **Effort:** ~3 days initial, plus ongoing maintenance.
- **Evidence:** Classic software testing literature; direct applicability to deterministic code; no equivalent for neural models.

**Investment #3: Metamorphic oracle harness + degradation sweep (Weeks 4–5)**
- **What:** Implement metamorphic invariance checks: re-render at 2x DPI (should extract same values), apply Augraphy blur+JPEG (extraction should still pass), apply ±2° rotation (coordinate-adjusted extraction should match). Run on each test case automatically.
- **Why #3:** Cheapest way to multiply your effective test count. Each metamorphic relation turns 1 test case into 5-10 test cases by systematically varying degradation and checking invariance. The oracle is free (re-render comparison).
- **Effort:** ~2 days for pipeline integration, 1 day per metamorphic relation.
- **Evidence:** DeepTest showed metamorphic relations catch failures missed by unit tests; exact oracles make this drastically cheaper than their DNN case.

### Low-Value Techniques to Skip (Under Your Constraints)

| Technique | Why Skip |
|-----------|----------|
| **Neuron coverage / DeepCover / DeepGauge** | Designed for DNN activations. Your system has no neurons. Use branch coverage instead. |
| **Evolutionary test generation (genetic algorithms)** | Over-engineered when you have white-box extractors and exact ground truth. Pairwise + branch-coverage-guided is cheaper and more effective. |
| **GAN-based synthetic image generation** | You don't need photorealistic images — you need COVERAGE of renderer styles. Programmatic rendering is strictly better: exact ground truth, controllable factors, no mode collapse. |
| **VLM-as-oracle (LLaVA/GPT-4V for verification)** | Contrains your design constraint 4 ("no VLM as sole correctness judge"). Even as a weak judge, VLM output is imprecise/confabulated for coordinate-level verification. |
| **Crowd-sourced human labeled real data** | You explicitly constrain to 100% synthetic. Even if you relax this, the labeling cost for coordinate-level ground truth is prohibitive. |
| **Formal verification / model checking** | Overkill for pixel-level image processing. Your extractors are not safety-critical in the aviation sense. |
| **Fitness-function-driven fuzzing (AFL for images)** | AFL-style coverage-guided fuzzing targets binary code with instrumented branches. Your Python extractors are better served by branch-coverage measurement. AFL's mutation strategy (bit flips) is meaningless for images. |
